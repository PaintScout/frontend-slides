A little tour of light

The Colors
of the Rainbow

Seven hues, one ribbon of sunlight bent through rain. Let's walk across the spectrum, one color at a time.

How it happens

White light isn't
one color — it's all of them.

Sunlight looks plain, but it's a bundle of every color travelling together. When it enters a raindrop it slows, bends, and the colors fan apart — each one bending by a slightly different amount.

That spread is called dispersion. Millions of droplets do it at once, and your eye stitches the result into a single glowing arc.

01

Redthe longest, slowest wavelength

~700 nm wavelength

Red light bends the least as it leaves a raindrop, so it always rides along the outer edge of the rainbow. It's the first color our eyes reach for — warm, loud, impossible to ignore.

02

Orangewhere fire meets fruit

~620 nm wavelength

Orange sits snugly between red and yellow. It didn't even have a name in English until the fruit arrived in Europe — before that, people simply called it "yellow-red."

03

Yellowthe brightest to the human eye

~580 nm wavelength

Our eyes are most sensitive to yellow-green light, which is why yellow feels so luminous. It's the color of sunlight at noon — and of nearly every "look at me" sign ever made.

04

Greenthe calm center of the spectrum

~530 nm wavelength

Green sits right in the middle of the visible range — the restful midpoint between warm and cool. It's the color we see more shades of than any other, the legacy of a life lived among leaves.

05

Bluethe color of sky and distance

~470 nm wavelength

Blue scatters easily in air — which is exactly why the sky is blue. Its short wavelength bounces off tiny molecules in every direction, painting the whole dome above us.

06

IndigoNewton's mysterious seventh

~445 nm wavelength

Indigo only joined the rainbow because Isaac Newton wanted seven colors to echo the seven musical notes. Many people can barely distinguish it from blue — a hue kept alive by tradition more than sight.

07

Violetthe shortest, fastest-bending wave

~400 nm wavelength

Violet bends the most, so it hugs the rainbow's inner edge — the last color before light slips past what our eyes can see into the ultraviolet beyond.

A rainbow is really a full circle — we just stand too low to see the rest.
