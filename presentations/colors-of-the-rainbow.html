How It Forms02

Refraction & Dispersion

Sunlight, taken apart by raindrops

White sunlight is really every color travelling together. When a ray enters a raindrop it slows and bends — and each color bends by a slightly different amount.

The light reflects off the back of the drop and bends again on the way out, leaving the drop fanned into a spectrum. Millions of drops, each sending one color to your eye, paint the arc we call a rainbow.

The Warm End04

Longest wavelengths · bend the least

Red · Orange · Yellow

Red

≈ 700–635 nm

The longest visible wavelength. Sits on the outer rim of every rainbow.

Orange

≈ 635–590 nm

A warm blend between red and yellow — named after the fruit.

Yellow

≈ 590–560 nm

The brightest band to the human eye, where our vision is most sensitive.

The Cool End05

Shortest wavelengths · bend the most

Green · Blue · Indigo · Violet

Green

≈ 560–520 nm

The spectrum's midpoint — the restful center of the arc.

Blue

≈ 490–450 nm

Short, energetic waves — the same scattering that makes the sky blue.

Indigo

≈ 450–425 nm

A deep blue-violet. Newton's debated seventh color.

Violet

≈ 425–400 nm

The shortest visible wavelength — it bends most and rims the inside of the arc.

A Curious Note06

Why seven colors?

The spectrum is smooth and unbroken — Isaac Newton chose seven names to echo the seven notes of a musical scale.
