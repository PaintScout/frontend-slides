A Field Guide to Light

Seven Hues · One Beam of Light

The Colors ofthe Rainbow

How a single ray of sunlight unfolds into the seven bands we name red, orange, yellow, green, blue, indigo, and violet.

02 The Phenomenon

What Is a Rainbow?

Light, bent and
split apart.

Sunlight looks white, but it is really every color travelling together. When it passes through a raindrop, the drop acts like a tiny prism: it bends the light (refraction), bounces it off the back of the drop, and bends it once more on the way out.

Each color bends by a slightly different amount, so they fan out — and the rainbow appears.

Sun behind you, rain ahead — that is when the arc appears.

04 The Warm End · Long Waves

The Warm End

Red · Orange · Yellow

The longest wavelengths bend the least, so they ride the outer edge of the arc.

Red ≈ 620–750 nm The longest visible wavelength. Bends least, so it sits on the outermost rim of the rainbow.
Orange ≈ 590–620 nm A warm blend between red and yellow — named after the fruit, not the other way around.
Yellow ≈ 570–590 nm The brightest band to the human eye, sitting near the middle of the spectrum.
05 The Cool End · Short Waves

The Cool End

Green · Blue · Indigo · Violet

Shorter wavelengths bend the most, curving onto the inner edge of the arc.

Green ≈ 495–570 nm The center of the spectrum — the hue our eyes are most sensitive to.
Blue ≈ 450–495 nm Short waves that scatter easily — the same physics that makes the sky blue.
Indigo ≈ 445–450 nm A deep blue-violet. Newton added it to make seven — a number he found harmonious.
Violet ≈ 380–450 nm The shortest visible wavelength. Bends most, so it lines the inner rim.
06 The Physics

Why Always This Order?

Wavelength decides
the lineup.

Violet
Red
Shortest wave · bends most Longest wave · bends least
  • Each color is a wavelength. Red waves are long; violet waves are short.
  • Bending sorts them. Shorter waves bend more as they pass through a drop, so the colors separate in a fixed sequence.
  • The order never changes because the physics never changes — that is why every rainbow reads red on the outside, violet on the inside.
07 Curiosities

A Few Wonders

Light keeps a
few secrets.

The rainbow you see is yours alone — it depends on exactly where you stand. Step sideways, and you are looking at a slightly different set of raindrops.

  • No two people ever see the very same rainbow.
  • Double rainbows have a fainter second arc with the colors reversed — violet on the outside.
  • They are full circles. From the ground we only see an arc; from a plane you can spot the whole ring.
  • You can’t reach the end. A rainbow has no fixed spot — it moves as you move.
